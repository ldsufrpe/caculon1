Of course  1  it is not surprising that if \(F\ne 0\) and \(G= 0\text{,}\) then

\begin{align*} \lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \amp= DNE \end{align*}

and if \(F=0\) but \(G\neq 0\) then

\begin{align*} \lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \amp= 0 \end{align*}

However when both \(F,G=0\) then, as we saw in Example [cross-reference to target(s) "eg_lim_rat" missing or not unique], almost anything can happen

\begin{align*} f(x)\amp=x \amp g(x)\amp=x^2 \amp \lim_{x\to0} \frac{x}{x^2} \amp= \lim_{x\to0} \frac{1}{x} = DNE\\ f(x)\amp=x^2 \amp g(x)\amp=x \amp \lim_{x\to0} \frac{x^2}{x} \amp= \lim_{x\to0} x = 0\\ f(x)\amp=x \amp g(x)\amp=x \amp \lim_{x\to0} \frac{x}{x} \amp= \lim_{x\to0} 1 = 1\\ f(x)\amp=7x^2 \amp g(x)\amp=3x^2 \amp \lim_{x\to0} \frac{7x^2}{3x^2} \amp= \lim_{x\to0} \frac{7}{3} = \frac{7}{3} \end{align*}

Indeed after exploring Example [cross-reference to target(s) "eg_zero_cancel_limit" missing or not unique] and [cross-reference to target(s) "eg_easy_0on0_limit" missing or not unique] we gave ourselves the rule of thumb that if we found \(0/0\text{,}\) then there must be something that cancels.
