Consider the limit

\begin{gather*} \lim_{x\to \infty} \sqrt{x^2+4x}-\sqrt{x^2-3x} \end{gather*}

Neither term is a fraction, but we can write

\begin{align*} \sqrt{x^2+4x}-\sqrt{x^2-3x} \amp= x\sqrt{1+4/x}-x\sqrt{1-3/x} \amp \text{assuming } x \gt 0\\ \amp= x \left( \sqrt{1+4/x}-\sqrt{1-3/x} \right)\\ \amp= \frac{\sqrt{1+4/x}-\sqrt{1-3/x}}{1/x} \end{align*}

which is now a \(\frac00\) form with \(f(x)=\sqrt{1+4/x}-\sqrt{1-3/x}\) and \(g(x)=1/x\text{.}\) Then

\begin{align*} f'(x) \amp= \frac{-4/x^2}{2\sqrt{1+4/x}} - \frac{3/x^2}{2\sqrt{1-3/x}} \amp g'(x) \amp= - \frac{1}{x^2} \end{align*}

Hence

\begin{align*} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} \amp= \frac{4}{2\sqrt{1+4/x}} + \frac{3}{\sqrt{1-3/x}} \end{align*}

And so in the limit as \(x\to \infty\)

\begin{align*} \lim_{x\to \infty} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} \amp= \frac{4}{2}+\frac{3}{2} = \frac{7}{2} \end{align*}

and so our original limit is also \(7/2\text{.}\)
