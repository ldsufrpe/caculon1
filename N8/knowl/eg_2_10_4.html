Exemplo 1.9. Derivative of a messy product.

Lets use logarithmic differentiation on the function from Example [cross-reference to target(s) "eg_DIFFsimpleToolsNB" missing or not unique]:

\begin{align*} f(x)\amp=\frac{(\sqrt{x}-1)(2-x)(1-x^2)}{\sqrt{x}(3+2x)} \end{align*}

Beware however, that we may only take the logarithm of positive numbers, and this \(f(x)\) is often negative. For example, if \(1 \lt x \lt 2 \text{,}\) the factor \((1-x^2)\) in the definition of \(f(x)\) is negative while all of the other factors are positive, so that \(f(x)\lt 0 \text{.}\) None–the–less, we can use logarithmic differentiation to find \(f'(x)\text{,}\) by exploiting the observation that \(\dfrac{\dd}{\dd x}\ln|f(x)|=\frac{f'(x)}{f(x)}\text{.}\) (To see this, use the chain rule and Example [cross-reference to target(s) "eg_diff_log_abs_x" missing or not unique].) So we take the logarithm of \(|f(x)|\) and expand.

\begin{align*} \ln |f(x)|\amp = \ln \frac{|\sqrt{x}-1|\,|2-x|\,|1-x^2|}{\sqrt{x}|3+2x|}\\ \amp = \ln|\sqrt{x}\!-\!1| + \ln|2\!-\!x| + \ln|1\!-\!x^2| - \underbrace{\ln(\sqrt{x})}_{=\frac{1}{2}\ln x} - \ln|3\!+\!2x| \end{align*}

Now we can essentially just differentiate term-by-term:

\begin{align*} \dfrac{\dd}{\dd x}\ln |f(x)|\amp= \dfrac{\dd}{\dd x} \Big( \ln|\sqrt{x}-1| + \ln|2-x| + \ln|1-x^2| \\ \amp\hskip2in- \frac{1}{2}\ln|x| - \ln|3+2x| \Big)\\ \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)}\amp= \frac{1/(2\sqrt{x})}{\sqrt{x}-1} + \frac{-1}{2-x} + \frac{-2x}{1-x^2} - \frac{1}{2x} - \frac{2}{3+2x}\\ f'(x)\amp= f(x) \cdot \left( \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{x} (\sqrt{x}\!-\!1)} - \frac{1}{2\!-\!x} - \frac{2x}{1\!-\!x^2} - \frac{1}{2x} - \frac{2}{3\!+\!2x} \right)\\ \amp= \frac{(\sqrt{x}-1)(2-x)(1-x^2)}{\sqrt{x}(3+2x)} \cdot \\ \amp\hskip0.5in\left( \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{x} (\sqrt{x}-1)} - \frac{1}{2-x} - \frac{2x}{1-x^2} - \frac{1}{2x} - \frac{2}{3+2x} \right) \end{align*}

just as we found previously.
